Email 1 — "quote tool": his seven concerns, the facts, and the counters

1. "Reluctant to move ahead when the first project is not complete… it is not being used."

"I understand it may be complete as per the scope, but as of now it is not being used."— Chip, 7/18
What's true The tool is complete per scope and every feedback round has been closed fast (see history above). Daily use has two real blockers, both fixable: mail traffic routing into the tool was waiting on O365 forwarding rules on their side, and outbound mail comes from the shared address he dislikes (item 2).
Messaging points
  • Agree with his standard, don't litigate "complete": "You're right — a tool isn't done until your people use it without thinking about it. Let's define done as 'in daily use,' and here's the short list that gets it there."
  • Present his own email back to him as the punch list, with dates. This reframes the email from complaint to plan — and shows (again) that feedback becomes shipped software in days.
  • Link the two projects instead of letting them compete: the knowledge system is built on top of the quote database — every quote and customer reply the tool handles becomes the memory the knowledge system searches. "Finishing the quote tool isn't a detour from the knowledge system; it's the first mile of it."
Watch-out Do not mention the forwarding-rules dependency as a "their side" excuse — it reads defensive. If it comes up on the call, frame it as "one config step we'll do together."

2. Emails from an unmonitored address — "the customer interface needs to appear to be human"

"I do not want emails sent to customers from an unmonitored email address… if the email can come from the person logged in that would be great."— Chip, 7/18
What's true (verified in code)
  • Nothing is ever auto-sent to a customer. A person reviews a quote and clicks send (routes.py send route behind a confirmation form). The background monitor only creates drafts.
  • But he's right about the sender: outbound quote emails go out as AEResponder@allanedwards.com — the same shared mailbox the system watches for inbound RFQs. Customer replies land in a parsing pipeline, not in a person's inbox. Machine-monitored, human-unmonitored. His concern is valid.
  • The fix is feasible: individual user accounts already exist in the tool. Sending as the logged-in person needs per-user send permission in their O365 tenant (SendAs — a Jackson Technical/admin step) plus wiring on our side. Replies then land in the salesperson's real inbox, with a copy retained for the system.
Messaging points
  • Concede fully and warmly — this is his identity point: "You're right, and it fits who Allan Edwards is. Quotes should come from your people, not from a robot address. Nothing has ever gone out without a person clicking send — but the from line should be that person, and I'll make it so."
  • Turn it into knowledge-system fuel: when mail comes from the salesperson and the system keeps a copy, the back-and-forth after the quote — the follow-up conversations Chip mentioned — becomes searchable company memory automatically. "This one change is also what starts capturing those follow-up discussions you mentioned."
  • Note the one dependency plainly: "needs one permission step in your Office 365 — I'll coordinate it with Jackson Technical."
  • Endorse his own phased instinct: he said "eventually we will move to an automated system… slowly train the customer." That is exactly the 80%-first philosophy already agreed — quote it back.
Watch-out Don't defend AEResponder as "actually monitored (by software)." His word "unmonitored" means "no human reads replies" — and on that he's correct.

3. "The tool is not even letting files sent to it be opened."

"It was at some point but now it doesn't work. a lot of people will submit a file for us to quote from."— Chip, 7/18
What's true (verified in code) The system reads attachments to extract line items, but the web app has never stored them or offered a way to open them — there's no attachment table and no download route. (Chip likely remembers opening attachments in Outlook itself.) So: not a regression, a gap — and a legitimate one, since people quote from the attached file. A fix is already being built (attachment storage + an open/download link on the quote, started 7/19, task 286).
Messaging points
  • "You're right — the file a customer sends is often the whole RFQ. As of this week, attachments are saved with the quote and open right from the quote page. No charge; this belongs in the tool."
  • This is the ideal first bullet of the reply: concrete, already fixed (or days away), free.
Watch-out Don't correct his memory ("it never worked"). Accept the substance, ship the fix, move on.

4. Requoting when scope changes — "will appear as a separate project and not close"

What's true (verified in code) He's right. There's a version record that increments when a quote is re-sent, but no real revision workflow: editing mutates the quote in place, and a requote today means a brand-new unlinked quote. No link between original and revision, nothing marking the old one superseded.
Messaging points
  • "Also right, and it matters for history: when a customer changes scope, you should hit 'Revise,' get a linked new version, and the old one closes as superseded — one project, full trail, nothing dangling open."
  • Sell the CRM tie-in: revision chains are exactly the "project history" the knowledge system will answer questions from ("what did we finally quote them, and how did it change?").
  • This is genuinely new scope — goes in the small paid package (see cost section).

5. Same project quoted to several bidders — "so historically we just change the name"

What's true (verified in code) No duplicate/clone feature exists; he did it by hand-editing, which works but is error-prone. A "Duplicate quote" button (copy line items, pick the new customer) is a small build.
Messaging points
  • "Good instinct doing it by hand — I'll make it a button: duplicate the quote, pick the other bidder, done. And the system will know these are the same project, which pays off later when we ask 'who else bid this job?'"
  • Small enough to bundle free — a goodwill line item.

6. "Custom field to quote oddball stuff or add comments… can't add new products without involving you"

What's true (verified in code) Mostly already exists — this is a discoverability problem, not a gap. Line items accept free-text descriptions with any price (not restricted to the catalog), and there's a dedicated "note" line type for non-priced comments. Product types can already be added in the admin screen with no developer. What genuinely needs a developer today: adding new catalog SKUs / new pricing-table rows.
Messaging points
  • "Good news — the oddball case is already in there: any line can be free-typed with its own price, and there's a comment line for notes. That's on me for not showing you; I'll send a 60-second walkthrough."
  • "Adding product types you can already do yourself in the admin. Adding new catalog items with pricing currently needs me — I'll add a screen so your team owns the catalog end to end."
  • A short screen-recording walkthrough here is worth more than a paragraph — it demonstrates the tool is deeper than his current picture of it, which directly softens "it's not complete."

  • One line: "Delete is already on the board and still free — if you'd like it sooner rather than with the first knowledge-system checkpoint, I'll pull it forward." Pulling it forward is cheap and reads as momentum.

Email 2 — the proposal: his four concerns and the counters

What's true Our own alignment audit (7/10) reached the same conclusion Curtis did: checkpoint 1 tracks Chip's stated wants closely (parking-lot lookup, phone dictation, warm intros), while parts of checkpoints 2–3 (all-five-libraries ingestion, weekly digest, some field-team machinery) are our expansion of his problem, not his literal asks. Messaging points
  • "Curtis is right, and I welcome the call." Say yes immediately and propose two concrete times — before July 30 if possible (see agenda below).

Cut every term he flagged as lost-on-me.

B. "I don't understand tech language… forgive me but im lost"

Messaging points

(The engine question is already answered in-thread; on the call, one sentence: "it reads your documents, finds the right ones, and answers with the source shown — like a librarian who never forgets.")

C. Monthly fees — "I get really on guard… our mtr portal has crept up to cost 1000 a month"

What's true The $200/mo is real cost, mostly infrastructure: roughly $130 of server/backups/storage (a bigger machine than the quote tool's because it holds the search index), a few dollars of AI usage per month of normal asking, and a small upkeep cushion. The quote tool's own hosting has run for months on exactly this discipline without creeping.
Messaging points
  • Itemize it and cap the story: "I'm on guard about creeping fees too — that's why the proposal has exactly one monthly line: $200, and I'll show you what's inside it. About $130 is the server and backups, a few dollars is the AI answering questions, the rest is my cushion for keeping it patched. It doesn't creep: if my costs change materially, the number changes only after we talk."
  • Use his MTR anchor for contrast, gently: one-fifth of what the portal drifted to, for the system that holds the company's memory — and unlike the portal, itemized and cancelable. "If you ever stop it, your data comes with you; it's your database."
  • Say yes to the centralization instinct — and make it billable discovery: "You're probably right that there's overlap to centralize. As part of the deeper dive (below), I'll inventory what you're paying across the portal and cloud tools and flag consolidation candidates. Wouldn't be the first time that review paid for the hosting several times over."

Fixed price, short (1–2 weeks), report he can hold in his hand.

Payment terms — he rejected net-15 and made a counter-offer

Read the counter correctly This is Chip negotiating toward the deal, not away from it. Down payments are not their norm — he invented one to meet us. On $19k that's $4,750 before any work starts, then checkpoint invoices paid ~45 days after each lands. Worst-case cash exposure at any moment stays under ~$7k of delivered-but-unpaid work, partially offset by the deposit.

Messaging points (option 1)
