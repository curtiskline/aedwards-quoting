The Allan Edwards Knowledge System

Putting forty years of customer relationships one question away.
Prepared for: Chip Edwards, Allan Edwards Inc. Prepared by: Devin · 918 Software Date: July 2026

The Idea

"I'm sitting in a Walmart parking lot. I look somebody up and I can see our entire history with them — communication, all our past work, people inside the industry who know them."

That's what this builds. A system that reads what the company already produces and answers questions about it — no forms, no database to maintain, no admin work for the guys who produce revenue. You ask in plain English from any phone; it answers and shows its sources.

First Checkpoint: Questions Over Your Customer History

We start on the quote tool's data — every customer, contact, address, quote, and line item — and load it with the archive of past quotes and RFQ emails we already hold. The first release is built around the new-salesman, Bartlesville, warm-intro workflow: who should I see, what do we know, and what should I say when I call. Then you ask:

"I'm going to be in Bartlesville this week — who should I see?"
Every customer near Bartlesville, with the context that makes the visit warm: when we last quoted them, for what, how much they've done with us, and who to ask for.
  • Talk to it. One field, built for a phone — open the ask page, tap the mic, and dictate, same as texting hands-free. Ask "who should I see near me?" and it uses your location.
  • It listens too. Learn something worth keeping? Say it into the page or forward the email, and it becomes a searchable company record immediately.
  • Every answer cites its sources, and your data never leaves your server — it runs on a private machine we manage for you, same as the quote tool, backed up daily.

It answers from what's loaded; the next two checkpoints widen it — the drive, then the field team.

The Work: One Price, Three Checkpoints

With that in hand, I can price this without padding it. The whole system is $19,000, fixed — one project, about three months, everything described here, and it goes all the way back: the full drive, the full history, not a sampled slice. You don't wait until the end to see it. The work lands in three month-sized checkpoints you can pick up and use as each one arrives, and billing follows the checkpoints, not the calendar. You'll be asking it questions in the first month; the full system is deployed when the third checkpoint lands.

Scanned paper is read by open-weight OCR running on hardware we control: no per-page fee, and your documents are never handed to an outside service to read or index. The inference that classifies documents is baked into the price, not an open meter.

Where This Goes

Your quote tool already reads inbound RFQ email on its own and files the customer and the line items — the first checkpoint loads the archive through that same lane, proven on your own mail. The second brings in the drive; the third puts it in the field's hands. You're using it from the first checkpoint on; the full system is deployed when the third lands. After that, the paper files itself: every new email, document, and credit application flows in the day it arrives. A credit application's three trade references stop dying in a drawer and become three warm introductions.

The note you BCC'd me the night of July 1st — the One Gas attorney, who's splitting off assets, the conference that matters — is the proof. That kind of thing has no home today. Here it does: forward it or say it into the ask page, and later ask "who do we know at One Gas?" — it answers, citing your own note.

Further out is the richer field workflow — push-to-talk from the field and a dedicated app if the web page earns it — and wiring in QuickBooks if the books prove worth asking questions of. Basic phone capture is already in the first checkpoint. That's later work, not priced here — figure $6,000–$10,000 when it's worth doing.
