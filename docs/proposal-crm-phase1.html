The Allan Edwards Knowledge System

Putting forty years of customer relationships one question away.
Prepared for: Chip Edwards, Allan Edwards Inc. Prepared by: Devin · 918 Software Date: July 2026

The Problem, In Your Words

"I'm sitting in a Walmart parking lot. I look somebody up and I can see our entire history with them — communication, all our past work, people inside the industry who know them."

Allan Edwards has decades of relationships — customer histories, past jobs, credit relationships, pricing context. Today that knowledge lives in a shared drive nobody can navigate, in QuickBooks, and in the heads of the people who've been there longest. You can tie a prospect to a job from 23 years ago on the fly. Nobody else can, and there's no way to teach it.

You've tried the CRM route — ACT, Salesforce, NetSuite, HubSpot — and every one of them failed for the same reason: they demanded admin work from the people who produce revenue. Your rule stands: you cannot give the guys who produce work more work just so overhead can have better records.

This system takes the opposite approach. Nobody fills out a form. Nobody maintains a database. It reads what the company already produces and answers questions about it.

What Phase 1 Does

Phase 1 starts exactly where you suggested: the Bartlesville question. We build a question-and-answer interface over the customer data the quote tool has already collected — every customer, contact, address, quote, and line item that has flowed through it. You ask in plain English, from any phone or computer:

"I'm going to be in Bartlesville this week — who should I see?"
Returns every customer and contact in and around Bartlesville, with the relationship context that makes the visit warm instead of cold: when we last quoted them, what it was for, how much business they've done with us, and who their contact is.
"What's our history with Washita Valley?"
Every quote we've sent them, what was on it, when, and the contacts on the account — one summary, with the source records linked so you can verify anything it says.

Three things about how it answers:

  • Every answer shows its sources. It doesn't ask you to trust it — each answer links to the actual quotes and records it came from.
  • No training required. If you can send a text message, you can use it.
  • No data entry, ever. Phase 1 runs entirely on data the company already has. Nobody's job changes.

Where It Lives

The system runs on a dedicated cloud server, the same arrangement as the quote tool at quotes.allanedwards.io — a private server that 918 Software manages for you. This one will be sized for what's coming: the knowledge system needs more memory than the quote tool's server has, and the upgrade gives it room to grow into Phase 2 without another move.

  • Reachable from anywhere — office, truck, or that Walmart parking lot — behind a login, same as the quote tool.
  • Backed up daily, same discipline as the quote tool's database.
  • Your data stays on your server. The system reads and understands your records using software running on that machine — customer data is not handed to outside services to be indexed or stored.

What It Accesses

Phase 1 reads one source: the quote tool's database — customers, contacts, addresses, quotes, and line items. It reads that data; it never changes it. The quote tool keeps working exactly as it does today.

That's a deliberate starting point. The quote data is already structured and already flowing in daily, so Phase 1 delivers the Bartlesville answer without touching anything else. The shared drive, QuickBooks, email history, and the credit applications in the drawer are Phase 2 — and the system is designed from day one to receive them.

What You'll See

  • The ask page — ask.allanedwards.io, a simple page where you type (or dictate) a question and get an answer with sources. Works on a phone browser.
  • The dashboard — a plain-view report of what the system knows and what it's been asked: how many customers and quotes it's drawing on, what questions people are asking, and anything it couldn't answer (which tells us what to feed it next).

Where This Goes Next

Phase 1 is the foundation, and the reason it's built the way it is:

Phase 2 — the paper starts filing itself. Emails, credit applications, shared-drive documents, and QuickBooks records flow into the system automatically. Think of how an order moves through Amazon — no person shepherds it; the process runs and people watch dashboards. A credit application arrives with three trade references, and instead of going into a drawer, those become three warm introductions the system can tell a salesperson about — the gold that today dies in an inbox.

Phase 3 — talk to it from the field. Push-to-talk from a phone: a salesperson leaves a customer visit, says thirty seconds about how it went, and that becomes part of the company's memory — searchable by everyone, forever.

Each phase stands on the one before it. Phase 1 proves the answer quality on data we already trust; Phase 2 widens what it knows; Phase 3 makes capture effortless.

Decisions We'd Ask of You

  1. Day-one users: just you at first, or salespeople from the start?
  2. Historical depth (for Phase 2 planning): your own question — "how far back do you want to go?" No answer needed yet, but it shapes Phase 2's scope.
