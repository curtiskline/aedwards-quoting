Proposal

The Allan Edwards Knowledge System

Putting forty years of customer relationships one question away.
Prepared for: Chip Edwards, Allan Edwards Inc. Prepared by: Devin · 918 Software Date: July 2026

The Problem, In Your Words

"I'm sitting in a Walmart parking lot. I look somebody up and I can see our entire history with them — communication, all our past work, people inside the industry who know them."

Allan Edwards has decades of relationships — customer histories, past jobs, credit relationships, pricing context. Today that knowledge lives in a shared drive nobody can navigate, in QuickBooks, and in the heads of the people who've been there longest. You can tie a prospect to a job from 23 years ago on the fly. Nobody else can, and there's no way to teach it.

You've tried the CRM route — ACT, Salesforce, NetSuite, HubSpot — and every one of them failed for the same reason: they demanded admin work from the people who produce revenue. Your rule stands: you cannot give the guys who produce work more work just so overhead can have better records.

This system takes the opposite approach. Nobody fills out a form. Nobody maintains a database. It reads what the company already produces and answers questions about it.

Where We Start: Questions Over Your Customer History

We start exactly where you suggested: questions like the Bartlesville one. Stage 1 builds a question-and-answer page over the quote tool's customer database — every customer, contact, address, quote, and line item. That database is young — the quote tool is just entering real use — so Stage 1 also loads it with history: the archive of past quotes and RFQ emails we already hold gets parsed into real customer and quote records, and every quote that flows through the tool from here on adds to it. Then you ask in plain English, from any phone or computer:

"I'm going to be in Bartlesville this week — who should I see?"
Returns every customer and contact in and around Bartlesville, with the relationship context that makes the visit warm instead of cold: when we last quoted them, what it was for, how much business they've done with us, and who their contact is.
"What's our history with Washita Valley?"
Every quote we've sent them, what was on it, when, and the contacts on the account — one summary, with the source records linked so you can verify anything it says.

Four things about how it answers:

  • Every answer shows its sources. It doesn't ask you to trust it — each answer links to the actual quotes and records it came from.
  • You can talk to it. The page is one input field, built for a phone. Tap the mic on your keyboard and dictate the question, same as texting hands-free. If you can send a text message, you can use it.
  • It knows where you are. Ask "who should I see near me?" and it uses your phone's location — you grant that once in the browser, and it's optional. Type a town name instead and it works the same.
  • No data entry, ever — but it listens. Nobody fills out a form and nobody maintains a database. When you learn something worth keeping, tell it: open the same page, tap the mic, and say the note — or forward the email. Either way, it becomes part of the company's memory.

One honest boundary: Stage 1 answers from what we've loaded — the quote archive and everything flowing through quotes.allanedwards.io. Whatever hasn't been loaded yet, it honestly doesn't know; the later stages widen that, one source at a time. Every source we add makes these same questions smarter.

Where It Lives

The system runs on a dedicated cloud server, the same arrangement as the quote tool at quotes.allanedwards.io — a private server that 918 Software manages for you. This one will be sized for what's coming: the knowledge system needs more memory than the quote tool's server has, and the upgrade gives it room to grow into the later stages without another move.

  • Reachable from anywhere — office, truck, or that Walmart parking lot — behind a login, same as the quote tool.
  • Backed up daily, same discipline as the quote tool's database.
  • Your data stays on your server. The system reads and understands your records using software running on that machine — customer data is not handed to outside services to be indexed or stored.

What It Accesses

Stages 1 and 2 read one source: the quote tool's database — customers, contacts, addresses, quotes, and line items. It reads that data; it never changes it. The quote tool keeps working exactly as it does today.

That's a deliberate starting point. Quote data is structured, we hold an archive of it ready to load, and new quotes add to it as the tool gets used — so Stage 1 answers these questions without touching anything else. The shared drive, QuickBooks, email history, and the credit applications in the drawer are the ingestion stages — and the system is designed from day one to receive them.

What You'll See

  • The ask page (Stage 1) — ask.allanedwards.io, one simple page that works in both directions: dictate a question and get an answer with sources, or dictate a note — open the page, tap the mic, talk — and it's kept. Works on a phone browser, behind your login.
  • The field team's logins (Stage 2) — accounts for the salespeople, with roles and permissions, set up so a phone stays signed in for a long stretch instead of nagging for a password every day.
  • The dashboard (Stage 2) — a page that refreshes daily: where the system stands as of today, what it's drawing on, who's been using it, what's been asked, and anything it couldn't answer (which tells us what to feed it next).
  • The weekly digest (Stage 2) — "here's what we learned this week": the little pieces of information that arrived — a forwarded note, a new contact, a fresh quote — gathered into one summary you can read in two minutes.

What The Data Survey Changed

That read-only survey is now done. We know enough about the volume, formats, and the split between born-digital files and OCR-heavy material to price this honestly without padding it. The result is a staged plan that keeps the work in month-sized chunks and puts a fixed number in front of each chunk before it starts.

The main cost drivers are exactly what you'd expect once you look at the corpus: how much of the shared drive is clean text versus scanned paper, how much duplicate and low-value material gets filtered out before load, how much legacy-format cleanup is needed, and the ingestion inference that helps classify and extract documents on the way in. That inference is not an open meter here — it is baked into the stage prices below, and with a lean model it stays in the low hundreds even on a heavy stage.

For turning scanned paper into searchable text, the plan leans on open-weight tools that run on your own server at no per-page cost: clean text pulls straight out of born-digital PDFs for free, and the scanned material is read by open OCR models that run in-house. A page only escalates to a heavier model when the confidence check says it needs one. This is cheaper than a dedicated OCR service — but the more important part is that your documents stay on your own server the whole way through; nothing scanned is shipped off to an outside service to be read.

How the Work Is Structured

From there, the work runs in month-sized stages. Each stage is a complete, working deliverable with a fixed price agreed before it starts — you see it running, use it, and then decide whether the next one is worth it. No long contract, no big bet.

For Stages 4 through 7, the range is there to keep the scope disciplined while the shared drive settles. Once the SharePoint rebuild question is settled, each of those stages becomes one locked fixed price before work begins. That keeps the model the same all the way through: scoped work, fixed price, no surprises.

Where This Goes Next

Stages 1 and 2 are the foundation, and the reason they're built the way they are:

The ingestion stages — the paper starts filing itself. Emails, credit applications, shared-drive documents, and QuickBooks records flow into the system automatically, one source per stage. Think of how an order moves through Amazon — no person shepherds it; the process runs and people watch dashboards. A credit application arrives with three trade references, and instead of going into a drawer, those become three warm introductions the system can tell a salesperson about — the gold that today dies in an inbox. All of it happens on your server, with our own pipeline — documents are never handed to an outside service to be indexed.

The note you sent Wednesday night is the proof. You met an attorney for One Gas, learned who's splitting off assets, got the name of the conference that matters, and came away with "find out who else has training centers" — and you BCC'd it to Devin because, in your words, this type of info doesn't exactly have a home now. You're right, and no CRM gives it one. Here, that becomes memory: forward the email — or just say it into the ask page from the parking lot — and later ask "who do we know at One Gas?" or "what did we learn about training centers?" — and it answers, citing your own note.

Further out — talk to it from the field. Push-to-talk from a phone: a salesperson leaves a customer visit, says thirty seconds about how it went, and that becomes part of the company's memory — searchable by everyone, forever. And if the browser page earns its keep, a dedicated iPhone/Android app is a natural next step. That is a later Phase 3 item, not priced in this proposal; the indicative range is $6,000–$10,000 when it becomes worth doing.

Each stage stands on the one before it. Stage 1 proves the answer quality on data we already trust; Stage 2 puts it in the field's hands; every stage after widens what it knows.

Decisions We'd Ask of You

  1. Historical depth: your own question — "how far back do you want to go?" That directly changes the volume inside the later ingestion stages.
  2. Stage timing after the shared-drive work settles: if the SharePoint rebuild changes the shape of the drive, we lock the exact fixed price for Stages 4 through 7 against the settled structure before each one starts.
  3. Day-one rollout: Chip only at first, or Chip plus the field team once Stage 2 is live.
