The Allan Edwards Knowledge System

Putting forty years of customer relationships one question away.
Prepared for: Chip Edwards, Allan Edwards Inc. Prepared by: Devin · 918 Software Date: July 2026

The Idea

"I'm sitting in a Walmart parking lot. I look somebody up and I can see our entire history with them — communication, all our past work, people inside the industry who know them."

That's what this builds. A system that reads what the company already produces and answers questions about it — no forms, no database to maintain, no admin work for the guys who produce revenue. You ask in plain English from any phone; it answers and shows its sources.

Stage 1: Questions Over Your Customer History

We start on the quote tool's data — every customer, contact, address, quote, and line item — and load it with the archive of past quotes and RFQ emails we already hold. Then you ask:

"I'm going to be in Bartlesville this week — who should I see?"
Every customer near Bartlesville, with the context that makes the visit warm: when we last quoted them, for what, how much they've done with us, and who to ask for.
  • Talk to it. One field, built for a phone — tap the mic and dictate, same as texting hands-free. Ask "who should I see near me?" and it uses your location.
  • It listens too. Learn something worth keeping? Say it into the page or forward the email, and it becomes part of the company's memory.
  • Every answer cites its sources, and your data never leaves your server — it runs on a private machine we manage for you, same as the quote tool, backed up daily.

It answers from what's loaded; the later stages widen that one source at a time.

The Work, in Fixed-Price Stages

With that in hand, I can price this without padding it. The work runs in month-sized stages — each a complete, working deliverable at a fixed price agreed before it starts. You use it, then decide whether the next one is worth it. No long contract.

Stages 4–7 show a range while the shared drive is in flux (you're rebuilding SharePoint) — each locks to a single fixed price before it begins. Scanned paper is read by open-weight OCR that runs on your own server: no per-page fee, and nothing scanned leaves the machine. Where This Goes

None of this is a leap of faith. Your quote tool already reads inbound RFQ email on its own and files the customer and the line items — the same kind of pipeline every later stage runs on, proven on your own mail. Stages 1 and 2 prove the rest on data we already trust and put it in the field's hands. After that, the paper starts filing itself: emails, credit applications, drive documents, and QuickBooks flow in on their own, one source per stage. A credit application's three trade references stop dying in a drawer and become three warm introductions.

The note you BCC'd me Wednesday night — the One Gas attorney, who's splitting off assets, the conference that matters — is the proof. That kind of thing has no home today. Here it does: forward it or say it into the ask page, and later ask "who do we know at One Gas?" — it answers, citing your own note.

Further out is push-to-talk from the field, and a dedicated app if the web page earns it. That's a later Phase 3, not priced here — figure $6,000–$10,000 when it's worth doing.

Decisions We'd Ask of You

  1. Historical depth: how far back do you want to go? That sets the volume in the later ingestion stages.
  2. Day-one rollout: you first, or you plus the field team once Stage 2 is live?
